Photography Tips

Photography while complex can be wrapped into three key items. Framing, Lighting, and Focus. While it is impossible to explain everything there is to know about photography, and not to mention that beauty is in the eye of the beholder, below are some explanations of each of these elements.

Framing:

Ever seen a photo where a person or animal is square in the middle of the picture and it seems off? Or ever see a photo where a person is cut off on the side of the photo? These are examples of poor framing. Framing is one of the most important things within a photo. Keeping a subject in frame and within what is called the “Rule of Thirds” will really help to increase the quality of your photos.

Now you might ask, what is the “Rule of Thirds?” That is an excellent question. The “Rule of Thirds” is where you divide the photo into a 3x3 grid (Most digital cameras and phones have the option to show this grid over the screen). Keeping the subject of your photo on one to two of the four crosshairs of the grid is how one keeps the rule of thirds.

So the best way to make sure you have good framing is to position your subject in one to two of the crosshairs show in the rule of thirds grid.

Lighting:

Lighting is also extremely important. Luckily most modern cameras have an auto feature that adjusts all of the camera settings according to the light in order to get the best photo it can. However, that does not change the fact that making sure you’re lighting is the best that it can be to achieve the best results.

Things to look out for with lighting:

  • Backlight
  • Extreme Darkness
  • Extreme Brightness
  • Natural Lighting

Backlighting is when a subject has a bright light shining behind them. This will cause the subject to become more of a silhouette and will wash out a lot of their details along with the details of the background. Avoid bright light directly behind the subject you are photographing, unless there is a specific affect you are going for within the picture.

Having too much light or too little light is also not good. While technological advancements can allow to shoot in very bright and very dark environments, not all cameras are equipped to do so. Knowing the limits of your camera is key. Too bright and the subject gets washed out and details are lost. Too dark and details are lost as well. Cloudy days are really great because it blocks the harshness of the sun’s light and creates a more soft light. Really sunny days work too, just be sure your subject is not backlit as explained in the previous paragraph.

Lastly, use natural lighting to your advantage. Personally I try to avoid using lamps, flashlights, etc. when taking photos unless it is at night. Natural light is just that, natural. It is what we see day to day and are eyes are accustomed to. For example, using light casting through a window, can be extremely useful when taking indoor photographs. Or using some light rays bursting through the clouds can look amazing!

Focus:

The focus is the last item to discuss about taking pictures. Focus is very important to draw attention to what you want to have attention.

If you are taking a picture of a grand valley or mountain range, you will want to have as much in focus as possible so that anyone that sees the photo can almost feel the scale of the valley or mountains and it will almost feel like they are there.

If you are taking a picture of a small flower, you’ll probably want to have the flower in focus with the rest out of focus to draw attention to the flower.

Luckily most camera’s and phones are pretty adept at pulling focus to what you are taking a photo of. It is simply something to be mindful of when you are snapping your shots.
